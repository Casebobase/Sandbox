Top five centers in history

1. Shaquille O'neal

Shaquille O’Neal, one of the most dominant centers in NBA history, played 19 seasons (1992–2011) with the Orlando Magic, Los Angeles Lakers, Miami Heat, Phoenix Suns, Cleveland Cavaliers, and Boston Celtics. He finished his career with averages of 23.7 points, 10.9 rebounds, 2.5 assists, and 2.3 blocks per game, while shooting an incredible 58.2% from the field. Standing at 7’1” and 325 pounds, Shaq overpowered opponents with his sheer size, strength, and agility. He won four NBA championships (2000, 2001, 2002, 2006), earning three Finals MVPs during the Lakers' three-peat alongside Kobe Bryant. Shaq was a 15-time All-Star, 14-time All-NBA selection, and the 2000 NBA MVP, cementing his legacy as one of the greatest big men ever. Despite his struggles at the free-throw line (52.7% career FT), he dominated the paint like few ever have. He retired with over 28,000 career points and 13,000 rebounds*, ranking among the all-time leaders in both categories. O’Neal was inducted into the Basketball Hall of Fame in 2016 and remains a larger-than-life personality in the basketball world.

2. Kareem Abdul-Jabbar

Kareem Abdul-Jabbar, one of the greatest players in NBA history, played 20 seasons (1969–1989) with the Milwaukee Bucks and Los Angeles Lakers. He retired as the NBA’s all-time leading scorer with 38,387 points (a record that stood for nearly 40 years), finishing with career averages of 24.6 points, 11.2 rebounds, 3.6 assists, and 2.6 blocks per game. Known for his unstoppable skyhook, he was a 6-time NBA champion, 6-time MVP (most in NBA history), and a 19-time All-Star. Kareem won his first championship with the Bucks in 1971, then led the Lakers to five more titles in the 1980s alongside Magic Johnson. He was a 15-time All-NBA selection and 11-time All-Defensive Team member, showcasing his dominance on both ends of the court. His combination of skill, longevity, and consistency made him one of the most impactful players ever. Inducted into the Basketball Hall of Fame in 1995, Kareem’s legacy extends beyond basketball, as he became a prominent social activist and author.

3. Wilt chamberlain

Wilt Chamberlain was one of the most dominant players in NBA history, known for his incredible scoring, rebounding, and durability. Over his 14-season career (1959-1973), he averaged 30.1 points, 22.9 rebounds, and 4.4 assists per game. He holds numerous records, including the highest single-season scoring average (50.4 PPG in 1961-62) and the famous 100-point game in 1962. Chamberlain led the league in scoring seven times and in rebounding 11 times, finishing with 31,419 career points and 23,924 total rebounds—the latter still an all-time record. He also won two NBA championships (1967, 1972) and earned four MVP awards. In his later years, he transitioned into more of a playmaker, even leading the league in assists in 1968. His impact on the game was immense, and he was inducted into the Hall of Fame in 1978.

4. Bill Russel

Bill Russell was one of the greatest defensive players and winners in NBA history, leading the Boston Celtics to 11 championships in 13 seasons (1956-1969). He averaged 15.1 points, 22.5 rebounds, and 4.3 assists per game over his career. A dominant force on defense, he never led the league in scoring but was a five-time MVP, thanks to his rebounding, shot-blocking, and leadership. Russell grabbed 21,620 career rebounds, ranking second all-time, and led the league in rebounding four times. He was the cornerstone of the Celtics' dynasty, excelling in clutch moments and redefining the center position with his defensive prowess. Though blocks were not officially recorded in his era, he was known for his elite shot-blocking ability. Inducted into the Hall of Fame in 1975, Russell's impact on and off the court remains legendary.

5. Hakeem Olajuwon

Hakeem Olajuwon was one of the most skilled and versatile centers in NBA history, known for his footwork, defense, and scoring ability. Over his 18-season career (1984-2002), he averaged 21.8 points, 11.1 rebounds, 2.5 assists, and 3.1 blocks per game. He led the Houston Rockets to back-to-back NBA championships in 1994 and 1995, winning Finals MVP both times. Olajuwon is the NBA’s all-time leader in blocked shots with 3,830 and ranks among the top in career rebounds and steals for a center. He won the MVP award in 1994 and was also a two-time Defensive Player of the Year. His signature “Dream Shake” move made him nearly unstoppable in the post. Inducted into the Hall of Fame in 2008, Olajuwon remains one of the most complete centers in basketball history.
